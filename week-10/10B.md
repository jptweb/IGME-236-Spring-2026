## The Big Idea: Garbage In, Garbage Out

Your prototype is only as reliable as the data that informs it. Two things can go wrong:

1. **Who you ask** (the recruitment problem)
2. **How you ask** (the question design problem)

Get both wrong and you end up solving imagined problems instead of real ones.

---

## Who You Ask: Watch the Convenience Trap

Sending your survey to roommates and Discord friends is easy, but it creates an echo chamber. Those people tend to have similar digital literacy and may tell you what you want to hear.

**Better approaches:**

| Strategy | How It Works | Trade-off |
|----------|-------------|-----------|
| **Passive recruitment** | Post on Reddit, forums, social media | Self-selected; may capture the vocal/frustrated minority |
| **Intercept / targeted** | Go to where your users already are | Lowest bias, but takes more effort |
| **Convenience** | Friends, classmates | Easiest, but highest bias risk |

You don't have to avoid convenience sampling entirely, just don't make it your *only* approach.

## How You Ask: Seven Common Pitfalls

These showed up in your draft submissions. Quick fixes for each:

### Assuming emotional state
Don't force a negative frame. Let the user bring up frustrations naturally.
- **Instead of:** "What's the most frustrating thing about myCourses?"
- **Try:** "Describe your typical experience navigating myCourses during a work session."

### Leading questions
If you ask whether a feature is a benefit, people will almost always say yes to be polite.
- **Instead of:** "Would this site benefit from a search bar?"
- **Try:** "How would the addition of a search bar impact your usage of the site?"

### Double-barreled questions
Don't jam two questions into one. It tangles the wires.
- **Instead of:** "Do you use Slack's organization features or is this the first time you're hearing about them?"
- **Try:** Ask each part separately.

### Binary questions / dead ends
Yes/No questions belong in surveys, not interviews. They kill the conversation.
- **Instead of:** "Do you prefer Steam to Epic Games Store?"
- **Try:** "How does your experience with the Epic Games Launcher compare to other platforms you use?"

### Scale problems
Use a 1-5 scale with clear anchors. A 1-10 scale has no true middle, and vague or hyperbolic endpoints ("physically impossible" to "perfectly smooth") skew results.
- **Use:** "1 = Very difficult, 5 = Very easy"

### Generalities
Icebreakers are fine for comfort, but don't treat them as data points. Every real question should trace back to something actionable.
- **Instead of:** "How did you get into PC gaming?"
- **Try:** "Walk me through the steps you take when deciding to purchase a new game on your PC."

### The echo chamber effect
Bad recruitment + leading questions = solving imagined problems. You might demand a total overhaul when the silent majority just needs a slightly better search filter.

**Objectivity is your only defense.**

---

## Four Steps to Fix Your Draft

1. **Purge the binary.** Eliminate yes/no questions from your interview script.
2. **Neutralize the tone.** Remove words like "impossible," "terrible," "frustrating."
3. **Untangle the wires.** Split double-barreled questions into separate questions.
4. **Burst the bubble.** Diversify your recruitment beyond your immediate social circle.

## Observations: Don't Forget This One

Interviews and surveys get most of the attention, but observations are powerful. Have someone actually use the tool you're investigating while you watch.

- Give them a task ("show me how you'd find your grades on myCourses")
- Watch what they do (don't guide them)
- Note where they hesitate, backtrack, or get confused
- Keep private data private (they can cover anything sensitive)

**Tip from class:** If an interview isn't going well (lots of "no, I've never tried that" answers), pivot to an observation instead. The person may respond better to a hands-on task.
