## Common Pitfalls (Don't Make These Mistakes)

### Interview Script Introduction

Your intro should be written **to** the participant — something you'd actually say or read aloud at the start of an interview. It should explain the purpose and why their feedback matters.

**Don't do this:**
> "The following aim to find user habits, frustrations, and expectations from Steam's mobile applications."

This is a sentence *about* the interview, not something written *to* a participant. It reads like an assignment description, not a conversation starter.

**Do something like this:**
> "Thanks for taking the time to talk with us. We're a team of students working on a project to improve the mobile experience for Steam users. We'd love to hear about how you use the app, what works well, and what frustrates you. There are no right or wrong answers — we're just looking for your honest experience. This should take about 10-15 minutes."

### Interview Questions — Write Them Together

Resist the temptation to split up questions among team members and drop them into a shared doc. Your questions need to work **together** as a coherent script — they should flow naturally, build on each other, and avoid redundancy. Draft and discuss them as a group.

**Ask about experiences, not opinions.** "What is your opinion on [feature]?" is vague and gets you vague answers. Instead, ask things like:
- "Can you walk me through a recent time you used [feature]?"
- "What do you typically do when you need to [task]?"
- "Have you ever had trouble finding [thing]? What happened?"

**Avoid leading questions.** "What do you find annoying about [app]?" assumes something IS annoying. Let the user tell you.

**Avoid hypotheticals.** "If [app] added X, would you use it?" — users are bad at predicting their own behavior. Ask about what they *actually do* now.

**Sequence matters.** Start broad (how do you use this?), get specific (walk me through this task), then open it up (what would you change?). Each question should build on the last.

**Bonus:** Consider adding a brief note after each question explaining *why* you're asking it — what data does this question give you? This helps your team stay focused and shows the grader your thinking.

### Survey Questions — Mix It Up

Don't make every question open-ended or every question multiple-choice. Use a variety of question types to capture different kinds of information. The survey is intentionally short (5-7 questions), so each question should earn its spot.

Think about what each question type is good for:
- **Multiple-choice** — demographics, usage frequency, feature awareness
- **Likert scales** (1-5, Satisfied → Not Satisfied) — measuring satisfaction, difficulty, frequency
- **Open-ended** — capturing experiences and ideas you didn't anticipate

### Initial Research — Actually Do It

One link to a Reddit post is not background research. You need to:
- Find **multiple sources** (Reddit threads, forums, app store reviews, competitor feedback)
- Summarize findings **for each problem** your team identified
- Look for **patterns** — do different users report the same frustrations?
- Note which **user groups** seem most affected

### Research Plan — Be Specific

"We'll survey people who use [app]" is not a plan. A strong plan includes:
- **Who** specifically you'll recruit (what types of users — casual, power users, new users?)
- **How** you'll reach them (which communities, group chats, in-person?)
- **How many** interviews (~5) and survey responses (~10) you're targeting

## Interviews vs. Surveys: Two Different Tools

Remember — these serve different purposes:

- **Interviews** = **qualitative**. You're having a conversation. Open-ended questions let you dig into *why* users feel the way they do. You can follow up, ask "tell me more," and explore unexpected responses.
- **Surveys** = **quantitative**. You're gathering structured data from more people. Multiple-choice and Likert scales give you numbers you can compare. A few open-ended questions can supplement, but the strength is in patterns across responses.

Both should be grounded in your **task flows and the assumptions you made** about pain points. You're trying to find out: are these actually problems for real users, or are you barking up the wrong tree?

## Accessibility Assessment

Also due this week: **Sunday, Mar 22, 11:59 PM** | Individual, pairs, or groups of 3

Pick a game your group is familiar with — preferably one you can actively play or observe during class.

### What to Do

1. **Pick 2-3 accessibility categories** (visual, motor, cognitive, auditory, onboarding, etc.) using frameworks from [Game Accessibility Guidelines](https://gameaccessibilityguidelines.com/) and/or [Includification](https://accessible.games/includification/)

2. **Analyze the game** for each category:
   - What does the game currently do?
   - What specific barriers might players face?
   - Reference concrete examples from gameplay (UI, controls, rules, feedback)
   - Be specific — avoid vague statements like "this is hard to use"

3. **Learn from existing critiques** — review at least one example from: [Can I Play That?](https://caniplaythat.com/) | [DAGER System Archive](https://caniplaythat.com/category/dagersystem-archive/) | [Access-Ability](https://access-ability.uk/)
   - Briefly mention one idea or approach from these that influenced your evaluation

4. **Propose at least 2 meaningful improvements** — describe the change, who it helps, and why it matters. Keep it realistic.

**Deliverable:** 300-500 word written assessment. You may collaborate on analysis, but each person submits their own copy.
